Install Claude Code and open your own site

6 min
What it is

Claude Code is a coding assistant that lives in your terminal (the plain text window on your computer). You talk to it in normal English and it edits your files for you. Regular Claude is a brain that answers you. Claude Code is that same brain with hands: it actually does the work on your machine. Your six sites already live in one folder called jxn. This gets you inside it.

Why it matters for you

This is the door to everything else. Once you are in, the rest of the track is you telling Claude what you want. Someone did the exact setup you are about to do, live, having never rehearsed it, in about five minutes. You can too.

Do this now
  1. Install the two free tools you need, one time each. Go to nodejs.org, click the big download button, run the installer. Then go to git-scm.com and do the same. Node gives your computer a thing called "npm", Git is how you get and save code. Restart your Terminal after.
  2. Open your Terminal (Mac: search "Terminal"; Windows: search "PowerShell") and install Claude Code:
    Type this
    npm install -g @anthropic-ai/claude-code

    (This puts Claude Code on your whole computer, so it works in any folder.)

  3. Get your project onto your computer. This copies your six real sites down from GitHub into a folder called jxn:
    Type this
    git clone https://github.com/Shanostylelol/jxn.git

    (Easiest alternative: ask Shane to send you the folder, or download the ZIP from the repo page and unzip it to your Desktop. Either way you end up with a folder called jxn.)

  4. Go into it and start Claude:
    Type this
    cd jxn && claude

    (cd jxn means "walk into the jxn folder." claude starts it.) It will ask you to pick a text theme, then sign in. Choose "continue with Google," use your account. It may ask "do you trust the files in this folder?" Say yes. You are in.

Checkpoint

You see the Claude Code prompt waiting for you inside the jxn folder. You are running it, on your own project.

Power move

You can also run Claude Code as an extension inside VS Code (a free code editor). Same tool, but you get to watch your files change live, in color, as it works. Worth setting up once you are comfortable.

5 min
What it is

Why it matters for you

Do this now

You open the preview in your browser and the headline reads the way you asked. You changed your own page.

Git and GitHub, in plain words

5 min
What it is

Two words you will keep hearing. Git is a save button with a memory: every save (called a "commit") is remembered, so you can always look back or undo. GitHub is a free online folder that holds your project and its whole save history in the cloud. Your project lives there right now, in a repo called Shanostylelol/jxn.

Why it matters for you

This is the safety net under everything. Because every change is a remembered save, you can never truly wreck your site: there is always a version to go back to. And GitHub is the bridge that lets your edits reach the internet (next lesson). You do not have to memorize any of it. Claude runs the machinery; you only need to know what the words mean.

Do this now
  1. Ask Claude to explain your own history back to you:
    Say to Claude
    In plain English, what is the recent save history (the git log) for this project? What changed and when?
  2. See where "the cloud copy" lives:
    Say to Claude
    What GitHub repository is this project connected to, and what is the difference between the copy on my computer and the copy on GitHub?
  3. Learn the one phrase that does the whole save-and-upload dance:
    Say to Claude
    When I say "commit and push," what exactly will you do? Explain it once so I understand it.
Checkpoint

You can say, in your own words, what a commit is and what GitHub is for. That is all you need.

Power move

There is one magic phrase worth remembering: "commit and push." Claude will save your change with a sensible description and upload it to GitHub, figuring out every step itself. You never have to learn the individual commands.

Check your work, and undo mistakes

6 min
What it is

Two safety skills. First, have Claude review your own page and catch problems (broken links, sloppy spots, an em dash sneaking in). Second, know how to back out of anything: interrupt a bad edit mid-flight, or roll a page back to how it was.

Why it matters for you

This is what lets you move fast without fear. You literally cannot ruin your site: every save is remembered, so there is always a version to return to. And Claude is even better at reviewing than writing, so it will catch things you would miss. Nothing you do here is permanent.

Do this now
  1. Ask Claude to audit a page against your own rules:
    Say to Claude
    Read the-drop.html and tell me anything broken, sloppy, or off: dead links, spots that look wrong on mobile, and especially any em dashes (I never use them). Just list the issues, do not fix yet.
  2. Learn the interrupt button. If Claude is ever editing and going the wrong way, hit the Esc key. It stops safely, nothing gets corrupted, and you just tell it what to do differently. Try it once so it is not scary.
  3. Practice a rollback:
    Say to Claude
    Show me my last few saved versions, and how I would undo my most recent change to go back to the version before it.

    You do not have to actually undo, just see that you always can.

Checkpoint

Claude hands you a list of issues on your page, and you know two ways out of any mistake: Esc to stop it live, and rolling back to a saved version.

Power move

When a terminal command throws a scary red error, the move is simple: copy the whole thing, paste it back to Claude, and say "what's wrong, help me fix this." A marketer with zero coding background ships real sites doing exactly this, error and all.

Own your masters and publishing

7 min
What it is

Every song is two things you can own. The master is the recording itself (the beat, the take, the production). The publishing is the song underneath it (the melody and the lyrics). They are two separate copyrights that pay two separate checks. Owning them is the difference between renting your catalog and owning an asset that compounds for the rest of your life.

Why it matters for you

Two hard facts. First, because you release direct-to-fan, you probably already own your masters, which most artists sign away. That is a huge head start, so the job is to protect it and never trade it for a shiny advance. Second, and this one costs artists a fortune: publishing money does not pay you automatically. You can hit millions of streams and collect zero of it, and nothing will ever warn you. You have to register your songs and raise your hand. If you wait too long (roughly two years), that money gets swept into the "black box" and paid out to the major companies instead of you. There is an estimated billion-plus dollars sitting there that belongs to working artists like you.

Do this now
  1. List every song you have released or co-wrote. For each, note who wrote what (your splits) and whether the publishing is registered anywhere. Anything unregistered is your money sitting on a clock.
  2. Set up the two layers: a PRO (ASCAP or BMI) as step one, then a global admin service to catch the mechanical and international money a PRO misses. Register new releases within months, not years.
  3. Do the rights-trap check. Take one contract you have already signed (a distribution deal, a sync, a sample clearance) and read it for the traps, or have Claude help you know what to look for.
    Say to Claude
    I'm an independent musician who releases direct-to-fan and I want to make sure I never sign away rights I should keep. In plain English, no legal jargon, give me a short checklist of red flags to look for in any music contract: anything that takes ownership of my masters or publishing, anything "for life of copyright," long lock-in terms, and advances that get recouped. Then give me the four questions I should ask before signing anything. I am not asking for legal advice, just what to watch for and when to get a real lawyer.
Checkpoint

You have a song-by-song list showing what is registered, a plan to register the rest, and a red-flag checklist you have run against at least one real contract.

Power move

Learn the one deal distinction that protects your asset: an admin deal (you keep 100% ownership, pay a fee for a set term, then walk away with your whole catalog) versus a co-pub deal (you give away a chunk of ownership, often forever, usually too early and too cheap). When any offer comes, your default question is: does this let me keep ownership? If not, walk.
